Articles

I think essays are a much underutilised form of communication. Most people I know submit that they haven't written an essay since college or, in the case of some more technical folk, high school. I think that a good essay is long enough to capture the majority of the thrust of a book in a small fraction of its length, yet short enough to force the author to make one clear point, since it teases apart the veil of length many authors use to hide behind.

In fact, much of the reason I'm interested in learning to write well at all is because I want to perfect the art of the essay: to be able to make bold claims incisively, yet with flair.

Here, I pick apart some of the most compelling or controversial pieces I've been sent or stumbled across, summarizing them whilst injecting my own views. Broadly, they're centred around technology, innovation, economics, the future, and how we're getting there. I concede that the sources are uniformly biased towards Silicon Valley at the moment, but I hope to broaden the scope of my analyses to other geographies and domains with time. As always, thoughts are very welcome.

  • Definite Optimism as Human Capital
    • Wonderful piece, excellently written. Centrally contends that American shift from a manufacturing to service economy has real implications for its technological future. Wang thinks having a clear vision for the future (not necessarily a rosy one) is in itself a type of human capital, like intelligence, and sees popular understanding of manufacturing and industry an important part of that. He ultimately calls for more investment in sci-fi that paints more optimistic pictures of technology in the future. Some novel points and a fascinating thesis, but I don't think its call-to-actions are realistic or indeed very concrete.

  • Why Do So Few People Major in Computer Science?
    • Given the rise of the tech industry in popular culture, and the associated increase in students pursuing STEM degrees, why is there not an outsized or even large bump in people studying CS over the last few decades? He swats away specious arguments like it being "too hard", students choosing majors based on employability, and immigrants taking jobs. I wasn't left with a single compelling answer to the initial question, but personally, I think it's mostly insular "hacker culture" (where programmers seem almost to be a cult of nerds) and people's lack of exposure to programming and CS in high school, which means many simply don't seriously consider it in college.

  • College as an Incubator for Girardian Terror
    • I submit that I don't know much about Girard beyond his theory of mimetic desire. The tendency towards mimetic desire, he argues, is most prevalent when people are similar in their outlooks and goals. And nowhere, Wang says, is a there a more carefully cultivated homogeny than on American college campuses, where students are hand-picked based on the twin sieves of competitiveness and ability to game the system. Wang suggests the solution, for a given college student, is to be either intensely mimetic (win at the social chess from the inside) or not mimetic at all.

  • Peter Thiel and Thinking For Yourself
    • Makes a very interesting point that the uniformity of career paths of elite college grads serves as empirical proof that colleges do not teach critical, independent thinking. There's also other evidence that most college students are not significantly better at forming arguments and reasoning critically when they graduate compared to when they start, but that's another issue. This essay mainly about innovation in Europe, and how, for example, German culture promotes independent thinking, yet Europe produces so few unicorns. Wang suggests this comes down to policy and cultural emphasis on stability and community. I agree, with firsthand experience showing how ambition and individuality are subtly but actively looked down upon in European culture. Status in Europe is divorced from work and money much moreso than in the states.

  • Mukesh Ambani Won the World’s Most Expensive Sibling Rivalry
    • Reliance, the company founded by the Ambanis, is to India what Dow Chemical, AT&T, Exxon Mobil, and Amazon would be to the US if they were a single conglomerate. Dhirubhai's long-term insight that yarn was a growing consumer niche came in very handy as he started a company around it and timed it perfectly. Dhirubhai kept an office building stuffed with employees whose sole purpose was to offer company-funded scholarships to government officials' kids. Isn't that crazy? Makes you see that success in Indian markets comes down to who is better at navigating a complex and deeply social regulatory environment, with Mukesh taking the stance of always posturing to line up with important policies, and getting to know officials before they rise to power. I'm also very intriuged by high-society in Mumbai; it seems like a fascinating counterpoint to Hollywood and Wall Street.

  • The Lesson to Unlearn
    • Our education system is centered around preparing for tests. In other words, learning to game the system. Graham believes startups are good because incentives are aligned: you only win if you make a great product that users want. I argue that the "greatness" of a product is never so binary: often “gaming the system” plays a crucial part in getting funding or distribution that characterizes the line separating scale adoption to fizzling out. Still, startup incentives are aligned better than most jobs and industries. It's fascinating that people so ubiquitously believe that gaming the system is associated with real work because that’s how you advanced in the metric-black-hole-riddled 20th century oligopolic society.

  • The Bus Ticket Theory of Genius
    • He believes that the capacity to undergo failure at a rate needed to produce great work is not a product of infinite diligence, but infinite curiosity. Genius is merely “aimless, but obsessive, curiosity about things that happen to end up mattering”.

  • The Refragmentation
    • The dvided American state is the natural state of things, not a novel world order. The 20th century US was one unusually held together by the twin threads of war and oligopoly. War led to the rise of big business (government contracts and legislative support), and technology was its downfall (eg by making supply chains feasibly efficient, so people benefitted from pros of modularity but not cons of miscommunication). Graham sees economic inequality (even increasing amounts of it) an inevitable consequence (and indeed, the natural state of things in a free market) of technological development (leverage increases over time as technology matures—the same amount of innovation in 1800 generated less value than it does with computers today, and thus made less millionaires).

  • Do Things That Don’t Scale
    • A classic piece that has become part of Valley lore. Making a startup succeed is in fact less about focusing on a problem and more about being fiercely attached to a general problem space, as well as aggressive user acquisition. Writing code in your room and feeling great about wrangling technical challenges is easier, more measurable and satisfying than getting rejected by strangers and asking yourself tough, honest questions. So don’t fall into that trap. Airbnb went door to door in NYC recruiting users. And this initial fragility is not unique to them. Going out of your way to delight customers (eg hand-written thank-you notes) doesn’t scale, but it helps you scale as word of mouth matters, and experience is part of product. The product/company test is a vector: it's not just the technology at any given moment, but sales/user acquisition/experience is something you can and must be creative about. It’s the yang to the R&D ying that pulls in funding. It’s part of the test that is starting a company.

  • How to do What You Love
    • Prestige is especially powerful as a magnet when you’re young—it causes you not to work on what you like, but what you’d like to like. You have to enjoy both the end (philosophically) and the process (most of the nitty-gritty)—how can this be reconciled with Cal Newport’s idea of being able to love doing anything, like a craftsman? Parents are conservative because they share all the risk of your actions, but none of the reward. Be careful about sticking to goalposts you determined a long time ago, lest you have a life chosen for you by a high school kid.

  • A Student’s Guide to Startups
    • Undergrads will find it difficult to succeed with a startup because they have a back-up plan: being a student. The paradigm and incentive structure that class projects promote (all work contributes to grade, work harder you’ll do better, there’s an unambiguous mark scheme and someone who knows all the answers) the wrong attitude about how you solve problems in real life. A startup spends many months building stuff to throw away. Someone with “work experience” takes responsibility for their life and knows the reward is not Ferraris, but the ability to stop treading the employment wheel. And people with this experience know that you’re rewarded for building things people want, not for working hard.

  • What You’ll Wish You’d Have Known
    • If sixteen year old Einstein or Shakespeare was in class with you, you’d never know they were bound for greatness, but that they were just another impressive teenager. When stuck choosing between two options, choose the one that preserves optionality, which is usually the harder one (math vs economics). Work hard on bleeding-edge projects related to your interests, and be the type of person where ideas can constantly and easily take roost, then act on these questions out of curiosity, not out of a desire to “make this your life’s work”

  • How to Make Wealth
    • The piece that changed the way I think about startups, and made me a lot less critical about entrepreneurs and Silicon Valley. Founding a startup is merely compressing the stress of decades of work into a few years, hoping to reap commensurate rewards. Graham claims that a smart hacker working at a small startup will create between 10-100x more output than the same hacker at a large company if they work much harder and are not constrained by corporate bullshit. The problem with large companies is that incentives are misaligned. Your value-add to the company can’t be judged individually, so instead it’s just averaged across all employees. This is a good deal for most people, but the top 5% get screwed, since they produce most of the value and get paid about the same as everyone else. Smallness gives measurability, and leverage comes from building technology: coming from the word technique, the way things are done.

  • The Most Intolerant Wins: The Dictatorship of the Small Minority
    • I've heard mixed reviews about Taleb, some friends touting his genius, academics branding his work as intellectual fast food. This article is about how complex systems exhibit emergent behavior, and about how a small, stubborn minority can get their way if the corresponding majority is flexible. A subtle and important statistical fact, but not wildly profound. I'm wary because, like Gladwell, Taleb uses copious amounts of anecdotes to make points about statistical concepts. Credit where it's due, though: his examples are compelling and are drawn from domains as varied as religion and language to dietary laws and pop culture. And his conclusion is novel: that for a society's ethical code to move forward, it need only be championed by a virtuous minority (3-4%). As much as many people hate blind SJWs, intolerance, he argues, really is the only approppriate response to intolerance, and social media is making it easier than ever before to become part of this virtuous minority of moral movers-and-shakers.

  • How Technology is Destroying Jobs
    • Starting in the 2000s, productivity was "decoupled" from jobs in a way we've never seen before. The science, technology and automation that enables huge increases in productivity is created by a small number of highly educated technologists who reap rewards. Despite enormous national productivity gains in recent decades, the median worker is no better off. In fact, they may be out of a job. In a Marxist world, this is the endgame: to have technology be such a huge lever that a tiny fraction of the population can sustain life, while the others enjoy the fruits of advanced civilisation. But in the free markets that have brought us so far, the lengthening of the technological lever simply means economic inequality will continue to widen, with no clear end in sight. This isn't doomsday, though. As certain jobs are obviated, other service jobs related to that obviation will pop up, they will just probably be higher skill than the original jobs. Jobs on both ends of the skill spectrum, like waiting tables and software engineering, will balloon, while repetitive office jobs in the middle, occupied by secretaries and accountants, will be gone.

  • Why Books Don't Work
    • Starts with the problem statement: books are media based on a faulty assumption that merely by reading lines on a page does a reader understand the material. Understanding material involves a lot of metacognition: knowing what is important and why, navigating common misconceptions, comparing perspectives, solving problems, and more. We've known for a long time that the bulk of learning happens in metacognition, yet books force the reader to bear this large burden of planning and structuring their learning all on their own. What if the tasks of reading and understanding weren't two separate things? Matuschak champions the idea of a new medium that shoulders this metacognitive burden for the reader.

  • Self-Deception as The Root of Political Failure
    • I'm very interested in the limitations of democracy, and I often wonder whether it's the the terminal system of governance, partly because I think there are scenarios where a strong, even authoratarian government, can be useful (c. Chinese industriliazation in mid 20th century). While behavioral economists put forth the idea that individuals often act against their best interest out of psychological bias, Cowen, here, instead says that poor political decisions by the voter are a consequence of individuals acting FOR their best interest. This is because their goal when voting is not in coming to a superior political outcome, but instead in expressing and reaffirming their identity through their vote. He believes that individuals falling victim to confirmation bias is inevitable given our biology, that "identity politics" is not some new, problematic status quo plaguing the 21st century. It is how humans have, and always will, act in democratic systems. Also fascinating to learn that self-deception and delusion may actually be evolutionarily advantageous as they give you more conviction in your actions and more confidence in your identity.

  • What Happened To Innovation?
    • I chuckled hearing this in the voice of SamA, who got rich building a location-based social media app. He defines major innovations as those that make you think back and say “I can’t even remember life without this” in the same way that I do with smartphones, even though I was around when my Mom had a flip phone. In 1990, only 2.5M people had access to the internet, and that’s since grown 2000x in 30 years. Same with smartphones. But despite the Apollo mission having the computational power of a modern toothbrush, we haven’t seen any major advances in the physical world of similar magnitude (aerospace, energy, biotech).

  • Premature Optimization
    • Startups often wrongly focus on incremental optimization that will, at best, lead to a 2-3x growth (email campaigns, A/B testing) when they need a 100x growth to survive. Focus on making shit people want, then optimize incrementally as you grow into the market.

  • You and Your Research, by Richard Hamming
    • Long talk given by a mathematician/computer scientist who did important work in coding theory, and worked closely with the likes of Feynman and Shannon. Outlines his thesis on what comprises a "first-class scientist". Though Altman cites it as influential, I honestly did glean much more than a handful of empty platitudes. Hamming mentions that there is certainly a component of luck associated with what exactly you accomplish, but none involved in determining whether you accomplish anything at all.
