A short note for those not familiar with operating systems. Operating systems like Mac, Windows, and Linux are long, complicated pieces of software that serve as the middle-man/mediator between the computer hardware that chip manufacturer's sell, and applications that Microsoft Word or Google Chrome, that end users interact with. It is in this sense that they "bring metal to life." They are responsible for doing everything from storing things in memory efficiently and safely (so applications can't screw up each others' memory), executing and keeping track of many calculations at once, and so much more. Because of this, the most popular OSs are often millions of lines of code long, and written by anywhere from teams of hundreds of people (at companies like Apple) to thousands of volunteers (in the case of open source OSs, like Linux, of which ArchLinux is one flavour).

In general, operating systems are one of my favorite examples of a technology that would seem like magic if shown to scientists one hundred years ago. They bring bare metal to life. Before uploading an operating system to a machine, we just have a mass of silicon and chips that respond in curious ways when you poke them with electricity. Afterwards, we have a machine that implements abstract computation on an electronic substrate, and can do things useful to human beings with the tap of just a few fingers!

The similarities between operating systems and feats of renaissance architecture run deep. The most salient one is in how they are both large engineering projects that involve a large degree of artistry and design. Sure, every large engineering project (eg. designing a sewer system or road network for a new city) requires thoughtful design beforehand to make sure there is no wasted effort -- this is part of good engineering. Both the Duomo and Linux kernel go further, though. For one thing, the two groups of people responsible for designing and implementing these projects are often obsessively careful about the aesthetic qualities of their work, going so far as to fret about little unseen details that make no functional difference in the final project.
